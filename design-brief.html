01 — Project Overview
What is Under Fire?

Under Fire is a single-page, scrollytelling data visualisation website tracking rocket, missile, and drone alerts across Israel from January 2020 to March 2026. It contains 142,837 individually recorded alerts. The site is built by Alfie Rees as a data science project.

The tone is editorial, neutral, and data-first — inspired by data journalism outlets like The Pudding, NYT Graphics, and Guardian Data. No political commentary; purely data-driven. The audience is general public following the conflict who want to understand the scale of what's happened through data.

The existing design uses a dark editorial aesthetic — near-black backgrounds, large serif headlines (Playfair Display 900), monospace data labels (IBM Plex Mono), and restrained colour accents. The goal of this brief is to iteratively improve the UI while preserving that core identity.

05 — Suggested Improvements for Stitch
Areas to Explore with AI Design

These are the specific UI/UX areas that would benefit most from iterative AI design work. Each includes a ready-to-use Stitch prompt.

1
Hero Section — Add a Background Image or Texture
The hero is currently text-only on a near-black gradient background. Adding a subtle, dark photographic background (smoke, skyline at night, aerial view of Israel) or a noise/grain texture could significantly increase emotional impact without breaking the dark editorial tone. The text legibility must be preserved.
Dark editorial data journalism hero section. Background: dark aerial night photo of city with radial red gradient overlay at 15% opacity. Foreground: large serif title "UNDER FIRE" in white at 9rem, monospace subtitle in grey, three stat counters. Fonts: Playfair Display 900 + IBM Plex Mono. Colour palette: #07070a background, #e8b84b gold accent, #d63031 red.
2
Finding Cards — Visual Hierarchy Improvement
The three finding cards above the timeline chart (pre-Oct7 / post-Oct7 / busiest day) are functional but could be more visually striking. Consider larger stat numbers, better contrast between the card types, or adding a subtle icon/glyph.
Three data stat cards on dark background (#0f0f14). Card 1: red left border, headline "Pre-Oct 7", large number "8,596" in Playfair Display serif, subtext in grey mono. Card 2: blue left border, headline "Post-Oct 7", number "134,241". Card 3: gold left border, headline "Busiest Day", number "4,000". Dark, editorial, data journalism style.
3
October 7 Section — Emotional Context Strip
Before the map, there could be a single stark text callout — something like "06:29:00 — The first alert. What followed was unprecedented." — that primes the reader emotionally before they press play. A horizontal strip with a timeline of the day's progression would help.
Stark dark callout bar. Background #07070a, left-aligned. Monospace timestamp "06:29:00" in red at 1.2rem. Below: large serif italic text "The first alert." in white. Then a thin horizontal timeline bar showing progression from 06:29 to 23:13 with dots marking escalation moments. Minimal, editorial, sombre.
4
Nav — Progress Indicator
A subtle reading progress bar below the nav (a thin line that fills left-to-right as you scroll) would reinforce the scrollytelling experience and tell users how far through the story they are.
Fixed navigation bar, dark background, with a thin 2px progress bar at the very bottom of the nav that fills from left to right as the user scrolls. Bar colour: #e8b84b (gold). Nav contains: brand name in gold monospace left, 6 navigation links center, "142,837 alerts" badge right. Height: 52px.
5
What Are the Odds — Result Display
The probability result (large percentage number) could be made more visually dramatic — perhaps with a circular progress indicator, a filled arc, or a colour-coded severity scale (green → amber → red) to help readers instantly grasp the magnitude of the number.
Interactive probability display widget. Dark card (#0f0f14). Centre: large circular arc indicator (like a gauge) filled to the probability percentage. Inside the arc: giant percentage number in Playfair Display serif 900. Arc colour changes from white (0%) to amber (#e8b84b at 25%) to red (#d63031 at 75%). Below: two comparison rows showing "All-time" and "Since Oct 7" values.

07 — Image & Asset Suggestions
Photographic Assets That Would Work

All images should be used as dark, low-opacity overlays — never as foreground elements that compete with the data. The data is the hero; photography adds atmosphere and emotional weight.

Hero Background
  • Dark aerial night photo of an Israeli city (Tel Aviv skyline from above)
  • Iron Dome missile intercept trail photograph (bright arc against dark sky)
  • Abstract noise/grain texture at 10–15% opacity over the gradient
  • Any image should be blurred (filter: blur(2px)) and darkened to opacity 0.15–0.25
Oct 7 Section Header
  • A dark, abstract dawn/sunrise photograph as a thin horizontal strip before the map
  • Should reinforce the 06:29 timestamp — earliest light before the attacks began
  • Apply: brightness(0.3) and a red gradient overlay at 20% opacity
What NOT to Use
  • No graphic imagery of casualties or damage
  • No political symbols or flags
  • No images that imply a narrative beyond the data
  • The site is strictly data-focused and neutral
Free Image Sources
  • Unsplash (unsplash.com) — search "city night aerial", "sky smoke abstract"
  • NASA Earth Observatory — free satellite imagery
  • Pexels — dark abstract textures, "grain film", "dark sky"
  • Generate with Midjourney/DALL-E: "dark aerial city night, minimal, blue tones, 16:9"
08 — Stitch Quick-Start Guide
How to Use This Document in Stitch

Stitch works best when given a screenshot of what already exists plus a clear description of what you want to change. Here's the workflow:

Step 1 — Screenshot any section mockup from this document (Section 4 above) and upload it to Stitch as your "current design" reference.

Step 2 — Paste any of the "Stitch prompt →" text from Section 5 as your generation prompt.

Step 3 — When Stitch generates a variant, check it against the colour tokens in Section 2 and type system in Section 3. Ask Stitch to adjust specific values until it matches.

Step 4 — Export the Stitch output and bring the CSS/design back to this Cowork session to implement in the actual index.html.

Note on "Pencil"
There are a few tools that go by "Pencil" — the most likely ones are Penpot (open-source Figma alternative, free), Pencil Project (wireframing tool), or you may be thinking of Framer or Sketch. If you can confirm which one you mean, I can tailor the export format — Penpot uses SVG, Figma/Sketch use design tokens, and Framer works best with React components. All the colour tokens and typography specs in this document will paste directly into any of them.
